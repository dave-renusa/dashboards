Vol. 1, and the through-line is no longer Virginia. The data-center backlash is now a national, bipartisan, multi-front fight, and last week it crossed two thresholds at once. In North Carolina, Durham became the first major NC city to pass a moratorium, and a Chatham County developer sued the county back over its February pause. In Virginia, the Digital Gateway saga moves to a Supreme Court writ panel in late May or early June. And nationally, MultiState's count is now 14 states with enacted local moratoriums and an Ohio statewide ballot initiative trying to bank 413,488 signatures by July 1.

Three things to watch this week. (1) The Pentagon's quiet freeze on 165 onshore wind projects — about 30 GW, including projects on private land — broke into the Financial Times Sunday. (2) Michigan's Court of Appeals largely upheld the MPSC's authority to permit renewables over local objections; expect a Michigan Supreme Court appeal. (3) UT Austin's water white paper dropped Friday and is going to reshape the water conversation in every ERCOT siting fight from here forward.

One Must Read on Durham, one Radar on the Pentagon, nine state bullets on the Frontlines.

Must Read

Durham Draws the Line

On Monday, the Durham City Council unanimously approved a 60-day moratorium on data-center development. More than 30 residents spoke during the public comment window. Outside city hall, a coalition called Stop Data Centers in Durham rallied for a 32-month freeze. Council members said they had initially wanted 24 months — but state enabling law caps any unilateral municipal moratorium at six months, and going longer would require a joint city–county planning commission process. So 60 days it is, with a longer ordinance to follow.

That mechanic matters. Councilmember Nate Baker was explicit: "Durham is the first, not the only, of North Carolina's major communities to do this." Read as a signal of the shape that's coming. The 60-day window is procedurally easy; the 24-month version requires coordinated city and county action — which is exactly what Durham County is now working up in parallel. If you have a project anywhere in the Triangle, Charlotte metro, or Triad, this is the template that's going to be referenced.

The Durham vote did not happen in isolation. Forty miles west, a North Carolina developer filed suit against Chatham County last week to invalidate that county's February 11 moratorium. ECO TIP West LLC claims an $11 million sunk investment, vested rights under NC § 160D-107, and an October 2025 zoning permit confirming a "use permitted by right." It's the Virginia playbook in reverse — instead of opposition suing the county, the developer is suing the county for caving to opposition. And it's going to be the case to watch on whether vested-rights doctrine can survive the moratorium wave.

The Takeaway

The center of gravity moved from rural counties to urban city councils in one week. City councils answer to a different electorate — denser, louder, more activist, with sharper media reach. Project communications built for a rural planning commission do not work in a Durham public comment room. The host-community benefits package, the data-on-record about water and energy load, and the engagement timeline all need to be calibrated for a city audience. And the Chatham lawsuit is the canary: developers are starting to fight back legally, which means counties are going to be more careful about what gets onto the record. Build the receipts both ways.

Also On Our Radar

Wind in the Holding Pen

The Trump administration has effectively frozen approvals on 165 onshore wind farm developments, the Financial Times reported Sunday. Combined: roughly 30 GW of capacity. The Pentagon, citing a review of "national security" criteria, is sitting on applications that in some cases were near final sign-off and in others wouldn't normally need DoD review at all. Some are entirely on private land. Developers describe canceled meetings, long silences, and a process that has, since August 2025, simply stopped moving.

The legal precedent is on the developers' side. The same "national security" justification was used to halt five offshore wind projects, and every one of them was reversed in court as unlawful — Vineyard Wind, Revolution Wind, Empire, and others are all back under construction. The American Clean Power Association is now organizing the onshore side. The realistic outcome is six to twelve months of project-level delay, not termination. But six months matters when an interconnection slot, a tax-credit window, or a PJM capacity-auction deadline hangs on it.

The Takeaway

For our wind-side clients in Iowa, Kansas, Texas, and the rural Midwest, the assumption now needs to be: federal approvals are an unpredictable variable. Front-load everything that doesn't depend on DoD sign-off — county-level entitlements, state-level interconnection studies, host-community agreements, and landowner outreach. When the freeze breaks (and it will), the projects with clean state-and-local paperwork will reach NTP first.

On the Frontlines
  • NCDurham, 60 Days and Counting: Durham City Council unanimously passed a 60-day data-center moratorium May 4 — the first major NC metro to pause. A longer ordinance is in development with the joint city–county planning commission; council members have publicly floated 24 months. About 6 GW of data-center demand is already in Duke Energy's Carolinas pipeline.
  • NCChatham Counter-Punch: ECO TIP West LLC filed suit against Chatham County last week, seeking to invalidate the county's February 11 moratorium and to affirm its 750 MW Triangle Innovation West project as exempt under vested-rights doctrine. The complaint cites $11M in sunk investment, an October 2025 zoning permit, and § 160D-107 protections.
  • VAWrit Panel Pending: A three-justice Virginia Supreme Court writ panel is expected to hear QTS' Digital Gateway petition in late May or early June. Plaintiffs (Oak Valley HOA, American Battlefield Trust) have until May 21 to file opposition. The state Supreme Court's decision whether to hear the appeal is fully discretionary; QTS is now the only developer still in the fight after Compass exited April 28.
  • PAConshohocken, Take Two: Main Line developer Brian O'Neill resubmitted his special-zoning-exception application for a 2M sq ft AI data center on the shuttered Cleveland-Cliffs steel mill site in Plymouth Township. Same project that withdrew in November under packed-room opposition. Township zoning hearing must be scheduled within 60 days. 1,200+ have signed an online petition. PA voters now oppose new data centers in their community 68% to 20% (Quinnipiac).
  • MIMPSC Holds the Line: The Michigan Court of Appeals largely upheld the MPSC's authority on May 8 to permit large wind, solar, and BESS projects over local objections, rejecting the bulk of a challenge from 75 townships. The agency had misinterpreted some procedural timelines, but the core siting authority survives. Counsel for the townships hinted at a Michigan Supreme Court appeal.
  • TXHill Country Hydro Headache: A UT Austin COMPASS white paper released last week — and amplified across Texas public radio Friday — projects Texas data centers could consume 3% to 9% of state water by 2040, up from less than 1% today. More than 400 data centers are already operating or under construction in Texas. The state Republican Executive Committee has called for a pause on "open-loop" facilities.
  • OH413,488 Signatures by July 1: Ohio backers of a statewide constitutional amendment banning data centers above 25 MW are mid-petition. If they qualify for the November ballot, it becomes the template for the 23 states that allow citizen-initiated ballot measures — including IA, IL, MI, MO, NE, OH, OK and most of our priority geography west of the Mississippi.
  • MEMills Vetoes, Locals Pivot: Maine Governor Janet Mills (D) vetoed LD 307, which would have paused data-center construction through November 2027 — even while conceding a moratorium "would be appropriate." The veto is now driving local communities toward ballot measures as the alternative path. Similar measures advanced in WI, NV, CA, MD, and AZ in the past two weeks.
  • NATLPentagon's Wind Hold: ~30 GW across 165 onshore wind projects sit in DoD review limbo, per the Financial Times. Same "national security" lever the administration used (and lost) on offshore. Expect ACPA legal action; expect 6–12 months of friction; expect a sorting between developers with clean state-and-local entitlements and those who needed the federal piece to paper over weaker county-level work.
