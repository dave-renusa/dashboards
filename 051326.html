RenUSA Leadership Brief

Volume 2 · Wednesday, May 13, 2026

Good morning.

The moratorium wave just changed shape. For the past six months, data center fights lived at the rural-county level — township boards and planning commissions passing year-long restrictions on land that was supposed to become wind or solar country. This week it landed in the state's second-largest city. Charlotte's City Council voted Monday night to pursue a 150-day moratorium on new data center construction, public hearing May 26, formal vote as early as June 8. Ten days after Durham. Sixty miles down I-85. The question was never whether the moratorium wave would hit Charlotte — it was how long the mayor's parliamentary maneuvering could hold it off.

The contrast with Utah could not be sharper. While Charlotte's council moves to slow down, Box Elder County commissioners unanimously approved a 40,000-acre data center campus — 62 square miles — near the north shore of the Great Salt Lake. Backed by Shark Tank investor Kevin O'Leary and Utah's Military Installation Development Authority. A dedicated natural gas power plant. Hundreds of protesters watching the vote on a livestream from a county fairgrounds because the room couldn't hold them. The project would eventually draw 9 GW of compute — more than double what the entire state of Utah uses in a year. Both stories are about the same question: who controls the process? In Charlotte, the city council just grabbed it back. In Box Elder, residents are racing to the ballot box before ground breaks.

One long Must Read on Charlotte, one Radar on the national-security play in Box Elder, five state dispatches on the Frontlines.

Must Read

Queen City Queues Up

On Monday night, Charlotte City Council voted unanimously to schedule a public hearing on a 150-day data center moratorium, set for May 26. A formal vote on the pause could come as early as June 8. The vote was striking for two reasons: its unanimity, and its reversal. In late April, Mayor Vi Lyles broke a council tie to prevent even scheduling a hearing on data centers. Less than three weeks later, the council moved without dissent. The political ground is moving that fast.

The specifics driving Charlotte's urgency are stark. City staff told the council that a single data center under construction near University Place could consume 1.2 million gallons of water per day during summer — roughly equivalent to 4,000 homes. Councilmember Lawana Mayfield cut to the chase: "We're recognizing that our state has created incentives for certain companies, which is putting a strain on our resources. We need to figure out a way for them to cover — basically, what is the cost of doing business in Charlotte and what is the cost to residents?"

The structural problem that makes Charlotte's moratorium moment different from Durham's is the zoning baseline. Data centers can currently be built in Charlotte entirely "by right" in eight zoning districts — including parcels adjacent to homes and in the heart of Uptown — without a public hearing, a community benefits negotiation, or any city council sign-off. About 6 GW of data center demand is already in Duke Energy's Carolinas interconnection pipeline. That load is looking for sites. Many of those sites are in Charlotte. If the moratorium fails in June, developers with projects already in those eight by-right districts lose no ground — the window stays open. If it passes, the 150 days are a clock: the city has until roughly November to build an ordinance, and projects that don't lock in before June 8 face a new regulatory environment.

The North Carolina geography adds weight. Durham's moratorium (60 days, passed May 4) is already in motion with a joint city-county process toward a longer ordinance. Chatham County is defending its February moratorium in court against ECO TIP West. Charlotte joining the list means the state's three largest metro areas have active data center governance fights underway simultaneously. Councilmember Nate Baker said from Durham what is now true in Charlotte too: "Durham is the first, not the only, of North Carolina's major communities to do this." That was ten days ago. He was right.

The Takeaway

The "by right" loophole is the strategic pivot point. When a data center can be approved without any public process, there is no natural moment to build the community record, set the engagement framework, or create a benefit package before opposition organizes. Developers in Charlotte's eight by-right zones have a narrowing window — weeks, not months — to get voluntary community agreements on paper. Once the moratorium passes, those agreements become either the model the new ordinance is built on or the proof point that unlocks expedited review. Projects that wait for the ordinance will be waiting at the back of a line. The Charlotte engagement calendar starts now.

Also On Our Radar

Streams Over Streaming

On May 4, Box Elder County commissioners unanimously approved resolutions authorizing the Stratos data center project on a 40,000-acre campus just north of the Great Salt Lake. Hundreds of protesters filled the county fairgrounds; commissioners retreated to a private room and announced the approval via livestream. Signs read "Don't sell us out" and "Streams over streaming." Commissioner Lee Perry explained the vote with a line that will be quoted for years: "Our vote today is not a vote for or against the data center — our vote is about personal property rights." Kevin O'Leary, the Shark Tank investor backing the project, called concerns about the Great Salt Lake "ridiculous." A Utah State physics professor called the project something that would "utterly transform this valley" over 30 years.

The project is backed by Utah's Military Installation Development Authority — a state body created to develop land for defense-related infrastructure — and is designed to eventually support 9 GW of AI compute, powered by a dedicated natural gas plant. Total investment could exceed $100 billion. On May 6, the Box Elder Accountability Referendum group filed applications to put the commission's approval on the November ballot. They need more than 5,000 signatures in 45 days. The applications are in legal review. If the referendum qualifies and succeeds, it would void the county's consent agreement — stripping the protections and conditions the county negotiated — without necessarily killing the project outright. Which means the strongest community-engagement argument against a yes vote is: "the agreement is the protection." That's a thin reed if the county's negotiated conditions weren't strong to begin with.

The Takeaway

The "national security plus state military authority" combination is the new preemption play. When developers get a state defense agency to formally sponsor a project, they're betting the federal-security framing overrides local opposition the way it's supposed to override local zoning. The judicial track record on "national security" overrides is poor — the DoD's freeze on 165 onshore wind projects used the same lever and has been struck down project by project in court. But the pre-litigation delay is real, and rural boards without dedicated legal counsel will feel it first. If you're working community engagement in a state with a military development authority, the window to build credible public opposition is before the state designation lands. Once MIDA or its equivalent is formally attached, the local governance conversation changes shape entirely.

On the Frontlines
  • NCCharlotte's Open Window: Until June 8, data centers can still be approved "by right" in Charlotte's eight eligible zoning districts without a public hearing. City staff confirmed one under-construction facility could draw 1.2 million gallons of water per day at summer peak. Projects with community agreements on paper before June 8 are positioned to be the model the new ordinance is shaped around. Projects that wait are positioned to be regulated by one.
  • ILJoliet in Three Weeks: The $20 billion, 795-acre Hillwood/PowerHouse Joliet Technology Center cleared Joliet's planning commission and city council in under three weeks — and the Illinois Clean Jobs Coalition held a press conference outside City Hall Monday to call out the pace and the secrecy. Joliet signed a confidentiality agreement with Hillwood during early discussions. IL House Majority Leader Robyn Gabel has promised the General Assembly will pass HB 5513 this spring, which would ban NDAs between developers and local governments, prohibit cost-shifting to ratepayers, and require clean energy commitments. Joliet is the case study the bill rides on.
  • MIThe Lansing Object Lesson: A month after Deep Green withdrew its data center proposal from downtown Lansing, WKAR's debrief with MSU professor Doug Bessette is worth reading in full. Changing the proposed project location, changing how it would be powered — even if operationally legitimate — looked like concealment to residents. "To the residents, that's not how it appeared. It appeared that there were secrets being kept." In the current environment, the standard for transparency is not "we told them eventually." It's "nothing changed after we introduced the project." Lock in your details before the first public meeting, not during it.
  • UTBEAR Has 45 Days: The Box Elder Accountability Referendum's signature drive is underway. The two referendum applications are in legal review; once certified, the group has 45 days to collect 5,000+ Box Elder County voter signatures for the November ballot. A referendum victory would void the county's consent agreement with Stratos — stripping negotiated conditions, not necessarily killing the project. For developers watching this: the referendum makes "the agreement protects you" the strongest possible argument for voters to leave it alone. Build a benefits package voters can see before the signature drive closes.
  • OHJuly 1 Countdown: The Ohio campaign for a constitutional amendment banning data centers above 25 MW is mid-petition, with a July 1 signature deadline. If it qualifies for November and wins, Ohio becomes the template for citizen-initiated ballot efforts in Iowa, Illinois, Michigan, Missouri, Nebraska — 23 states with initiative processes, most of them in our priority geography. Watch the signature pace in June: if they're close, expect coordinated industry counter-messaging. If they're clearly going to miss, expect developers to pivot from defense back to offense fast.
