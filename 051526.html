RenUSA Leadership Brief

Volume 3 · Friday, May 15, 2026

Friday morning, team.

The moratorium wave just changed scale. For three weeks we have been tracking county boards and city councils — Durham, Charlotte, Box Elder, the Ohio township circuits. This week it went up a level. New York lawmakers and advocates rallied at the state Capitol on Wednesday to push for a three-year statewide moratorium on data center construction — not a city pause, not a county restriction, but a full legislative stop while regulators study the damage. The same day, Socorro County, New Mexico approved a moratorium ordinance process tied to a proposed 10,000-acre campus. On Thursday, more than 60 environmental, labor, and community groups asked New Jersey Governor Sherrill to invoke emergency powers to halt all new data center construction in the state — not legislation, emergency action. Three days. Three states. Three different instruments of opposition.

The through line is acceleration. Opposition coalitions learned from the last six months that waiting for developers to introduce projects, then fighting them on the planning commission docket, is the slow road. The new play is getting ahead of the pipeline entirely — moratoriums first, then ordinances, then community agreements from a position of strength. The NJ coalition's letter explicitly said 65 percent of registered voters support banning new data centers until more power plants can be built. When your opposition can move poll numbers before ground breaks, the engagement calendar has to start earlier than it did last year.

In Michigan, a different kind of ruling changed the map for developers. The state Appeals Court upheld the MPSC's authority to approve wind, solar, and storage projects over local governments' objections — and 75 townships are now considering their next move. That's the week in one sentence: the top-down preemption play won in Michigan, and the bottom-up coalition play is winning everywhere else.

Also On Our Radar
  • Albany Aims High: Supporters of a proposed three-year statewide data center moratorium rallied at the New York State Capitol on Wednesday, May 13. The legislation has sponsors in both the Assembly and the Senate, led by Sen. Kristin Gonzalez. The hook: projected data center electricity demand would more than double all New York household consumption combined. The bill would freeze approvals while the DEC and Public Service Commission study cumulative impacts. A resident from Alabama, NY — site of a proposed $19.4 billion, 2.2 million-square-foot campus — showed up to testify about infrasound. The Data Center Coalition is pushing back, but the coalition behind this bill is the same model that built momentum in NJ. If both states move simultaneously, the northeast becomes a template.
  • Jersey Makes It an Emergency: More than 60 organizations sent a formal letter to Gov. Mikie Sherrill on Thursday urging emergency executive action to halt new data center approvals statewide. The PFAS angle is new and sharp: some cooling systems use fluorinated substances linked to forever-chemical contamination, and several Pinelands municipalities — Pemberton, Monroe, North Hanover — have already passed local bans. A FDU poll shows 65 percent of registered voters support a moratorium until the grid catches up. The petition is approaching 7,500 signatures. The legal hook is Sherrill's own PFAS pledge: the groups are arguing she already committed to PFAS mitigation and can't exempt data center cooling systems from that promise. Framing an industry fight as a commitment-consistency play against the governor is advanced tactics.
  • Hill Country Holds: Hill County, Texas commissioners voted 3-2 Tuesday to impose a one-year moratorium on data centers in unincorporated county areas. Texas counties lack formal zoning authority — this moratorium lives in a legal gray zone — but the vote was a political signal. Resident Tim Lyness was explicit: "I know the moratorium within the state of Texas at a county level is probably a stretch. But we need more counties to get in here with us and send it back to the state." That is the strategy statement of the opposition coalition movement in a single sentence: use county-level action to build pressure for state preemption, then flip preemption against developers the same way developers used it against townships in Michigan. Watch which Texas counties pick this up in the next 30 days.
  • Socorro Slow-Roll: Socorro County, New Mexico commissioners approved the ordinance publication process on May 12, setting up a June 9 vote on a one-year moratorium on data centers and associated infrastructure in unincorporated areas. The trigger is a proposed 10,000-acre data center campus whose developers had previously announced plans for a dedicated on-site power generation facility. A blue ribbon community committee would be appointed to develop longer-term ordinances if the moratorium passes. New Mexico is priority-geography. Socorro County is on our board.
  • Lansing Landmark: The Michigan Court of Appeals issued a unanimous ruling May 8 largely upholding the Michigan Public Service Commission's authority to approve large wind, solar, and energy storage projects over township objections. The lawsuit was filed by 75 townships in 2024. The court rejected their Administrative Procedures Act challenge but found two narrower procedural misinterpretations — meaning the MPSC's core authority stands but has to fine-tune its process. The townships' attorney said they are "still digesting" the ruling and considering a Michigan Supreme Court appeal. Until that happens, the MPSC path is open: a developer denied at the local level can go to Lansing. That changes the risk calculus on every contested project in Michigan.
On the Frontlines
  • OHRichland Rejects: In Ohio's May 5 primary, voters in 11 of Richland County's 18 townships ratified a commissioner-imposed solar and wind farm ban, 53 to 47 percent. The vote was triggered by SB 52's citizen referendum mechanism — the same mechanism whose Ohio constitutional amendment version is now circulating statewide with a July 1 signature deadline. Richland County is now the 28th Ohio county to issue some form of renewable energy restriction. One in three Ohio counties has one. The Sabin Center at Columbia counts more than 450 such restrictions nationally across 44 states. Richland isn't an outlier. It's a data point in a pattern.
  • MIPlainwell's Planning Moment: Copenhagen Infrastructure Partners' proposed BESS off Riverview Drive in Plainwell, Allegan County, heads to Gun Plain Township's planning commission for a site plan vote on May 20. Residents have placed yard signs opposing the project, and the Gun Plain Charter Township Citizens' Coalition held an opposition meeting Wednesday. Township Supervisor Mike Vandenberg acknowledged the confusion: many residents think BESS and data centers are the same thing. That confusion is the project's biggest vulnerability right now — not the safety record, not the grid argument. If CIP gets the planning commission denial, they can go to the MPSC under the new siting law. The question is whether local opposition is enough to make that state-level fight worth having.
  • NCCharlotte's Clock: Charlotte City Council's public hearing on the 150-day data center moratorium is set for May 26, with a formal vote expected June 8. Projects in Charlotte's eight by-right zoning districts have until then to complete voluntary community agreements before the ordinance process begins. City staff confirmed that a single under-construction facility could draw 1.2 million gallons of water per day at summer peak. Projects that lock in benefit packages before June 8 have a reasonable shot at being the model the new ordinance is built around. Projects that wait are positioned to be regulated by one. Three weeks left.
  • TXHill's Legal Horizon: The Hill County moratorium has a structural problem its advocates know about: Texas counties do not have formal zoning authority, and the moratorium has no explicit enforcement mechanism against a developer who simply proceeds. The county is betting that the chilling effect, plus the year of research time, is worth the legal ambiguity. State Rep. involvement is the only real path to a durable restriction — which is precisely what Tim Lyness asked for publicly on Tuesday. If the Texas Legislature picks this up before the next session, Hill County will be the case study. That's a useful position to be building, even if the moratorium itself doesn't hold.
Plays of the Week
  • NJ CoalitionThe Emergency Powers Frame: Going directly to a governor for emergency executive action — bypassing a legislative timeline entirely — is an escalation most opposition coalitions don't attempt because it looks overreaching. The New Jersey coalition made it land by doing three things at once: (1) citing a specific poll number (65%) that puts the governor on the wrong side of her own voters, (2) tying the ask to a PFAS commitment Sherrill already made publicly, and (3) deploying 60+ named organizations simultaneously so no single group takes the political hit. The framing isn't "we oppose data centers" — it's "the governor said she'd protect us from PFAS and these facilities are PFAS sources." That is a constituency-accountability play, not an advocacy play, and it's much harder to dismiss. Worth studying before the next contested project in a state with an active PFAS story.
  • NY OpponentsThe Research Hook: Cheryl Cordes from Alabama, New York testified about infrasound — low-frequency noise that data center cooling systems generate — at the state capitol rally Wednesday. Infrasound is not a fabricated concern: the scientific literature on industrial infrasound and health effects is real, contested, and hard to dismiss in a public hearing. Deploying a resident with a specific, science-adjacent grievance alongside a state senator's energy math ("double all New York households") is a two-audience play: the senator's argument moves legislators, the resident's argument moves sympathetic coverage. Both appeared in the same Spectrum News story. That's coordinated message architecture, whether or not it was consciously designed as such.
  • Hill County ResidentsThe Property Rights Flip: Texas conservative politics runs on property rights. Developers have long used the property rights frame to argue landowners should be free to sell or lease to data center companies without county interference. Hill County resident Tim Lyness flipped it: "Property rights stop at your property line." That's a bumper-sticker reframe that uses the opposition's own language to limit the opposition's argument. When a developer says "property rights," the counter is now established: sure, yours end at the fence line. Watch for this phrase in Texas county hearings through the end of the year. If it spreads, the developer property-rights argument in rural Texas becomes significantly harder to deploy.
  • Michigan MPSCThe Preemption Path: The Michigan Appeals Court ruling isn't just a legal result — it is a publicly announced developer playbook. If your project gets denied at the local level in Michigan, you now have a confirmed path to state approval through the MPSC. The Plainwell BESS situation (planning commission vote May 20) is the first live test of whether local opposition will feel this shift. The risk is that communities read the MPSC path as a signal that local engagement doesn't matter — and stop coming to the table. The strategic value of the state route depends on projects still doing the local work, because the community record still shapes the MPSC proceeding. Using preemption as a threat instead of a backup is the fastest way to make every project fight harder than the last one.
