If there's a single thread running through this week, it's the price of skipping the front end. Virginia just paid it — Compass Datacenters walked away from the Prince William Digital Gateway after a Court of Appeals ruling pinned the rezoning on a procedural failure that was avoidable two years ago. North Carolina's opposition is now organized enough that WUNC is calling it a midterm issue. Battery storage is taking incoming in New York and Washington. And the Mid-Atlantic transmission build is starting to look like nine simultaneous county-line knife fights.

Three takeaways below. Then the radar, the frontlines (sixteen bullets across our priority states), and what our peers are up to.

Must Read

The Gateway Closes

Compass Datacenters has formally walked away from the Prince William Digital Gateway — a 2,100-acre, multi-gigawatt corridor that was, until two months ago, the largest data center play on the East Coast. The exit follows the Virginia Court of Appeals' March 31 ruling invalidating the county's 2023 rezoning, and the Board of Supervisors' April 14 decision to stop defending it. The case is now heading to the Virginia Supreme Court — but the developer isn't waiting around.

The opposition won on procedure, not merits. The court found Prince William failed to meet public-notice requirements before the rezoning vote — a documentation failure, not a substantive one. That's the choke point opposition counsel across the country is now drilling into: comprehensive plan amendments, notice timelines, the paper trail of every staff conversation. Two years of capital and political work undone by a meeting agenda that was posted wrong.

The political wallpaper around it has shifted just as fast. A new statewide poll has Virginia voter support for new data centers collapsing from 69% in 2023 to 35% in 2026. Senator Danica Roem has filed a sweeping crackdown for the 2027 session — industrial-only siting and a kill-switch on the sales-tax exemption. Loudoun, the world's data-center capital, ended by-right development in March 2025, meaning every project now requires legislative special exception. The state has gone from rolling out the red carpet to rolling it up, in eighteen months.

The Takeaway

Virginia is the model and the warning. The legal vulnerability isn't whether the project is good; it's whether the receipts are clean. Comprehensive plan compliance, notice deadlines, and the documentation of every public-engagement step are now the difference between a permit and a court loss. Build the paper trail before the rezoning vote, not after. The states watching this — North Carolina, Indiana, Georgia, Iowa, Texas — are all watching it.

Also On Our Radar

Tar Heel Tipping Point

If Virginia is the warning, North Carolina is the live test. WUNC and VPM reported this week that data center opposition has gone from scattered objections to a statewide organizing network — one that's now showing up in Republican primary mailers, county commission elections, and a live lawsuit in Stokes County over the proposed 1,800-acre Project Delta site near the Dan River.

The grievance set rhymes with Virginia's: utility bill spillover, water draw, on-site gas generation, noise from cooling fans, and the conversion of rural land to industrial use without permanent jobs. What's different is the speed at which it's coordinating across counties — and that NPR is now framing it as a midterm-grade voter issue. Layer on the North Carolina Utilities Commission's surprise decision to pause Duke Energy's 2026 solar procurement, and you have a state where the supply side and the demand side are both getting harder at the same time.

The Takeaway

Stokes is the leading edge — but the playbook (lawsuit + organized comms + cross-county coordination) is portable. If you have a project anywhere in the Carolinas, Indiana, or Georgia in the next 18 months, assume the opposition has read the Virginia transcript and is hiring the lawyers who wrote it.

On the Frontlines
  • VACompass Lost: Compass Datacenters formally exits the Prince William Digital Gateway; QTS and Blackstone-backed entities are still in the Va Supreme Court fight.
  • VAValley of the Pushback: Early outreach for the Valley Link transmission project is finding organized opposition across nine Central Virginia counties — Appomattox, Buckingham, Campbell, Culpeper, Fluvanna, Goochland, Louisa, Orange, and Spotsylvania — ahead of the SCC route filing in September.
  • NCProject Delta, Phase Lawsuit: Stokes County residents filed suit to stop the 1,800-acre Project Delta data center near the Dan River; the case is now central to a statewide test of zoning-amendment process.
  • NCSolar Pause Button: The North Carolina Utilities Commission ordered Duke Energy to pause its 2026 competitive solar procurement; SELC has filed for reconsideration.
  • INSentinel Stalls: The Sentinel data center developer postponed its Indiana rezoning vote to May after hundreds of residents packed a high-school auditorium to oppose converting agricultural land. Several Indiana counties have already imposed local data-center moratoriums.
  • IAHawkeye Hardline: Linn County, Iowa adopted some of the country's strictest data-center zoning rules this spring — and residents are already saying it doesn't go far enough.
  • IADavenport Drags On: Davenport's solar-farm ordinance moratorium enters its tenth month; council briefed last month with no movement, expiration set for late June.
  • UTWonder What?: The Box Elder County Commission unanimously approved Kevin O'Leary's "Wonder Valley" data center on May 4, in a room packed with hundreds of vocal protesters. The MIDA structure neutralized local zoning; that's a tactic to watch.
  • TXSan Marcos Slow-Roll: San Marcos City Council paused a $1.5B, 200-acre data center proposal after residents flagged grid strain in an area already prone to brownouts.
  • NYPlattekill Pulls the Plug: The Plattekill Town Board voted unanimously to ban large-scale BESS, citing fire risk and toxic-release concerns. Staten Island BP Vito Fossella has called for a borough-wide residential BESS moratorium; the Ulster County GOP caucus is demanding a countywide pause.
  • NYTown of Alabama, $20B Asks: The Town of Alabama held a public hearing on a $20 billion Stream Data Centers proposal at the Stamp Industrial Park — a useful case study in how host-community benefits can be structured around demand at this scale.
  • WAKitsap Cools Off: Kitsap County's 365-day BESS moratorium runs into May 2026; expect a contested ordinance fight on the back end.
  • CASafety Chain Tightens: California's SB 283 (Clean Energy Safety Act) now requires documented pre-application fire-authority consultation and a pre-energization inspection — fallout from the December Warwick, NY thermal-runaway fire is reshaping siting expectations nationwide.
  • CACPUC's May 14: The California PUC's May 14 business meeting will consider a community-solar pricing framework that PV Magazine warns could "virtually ensure no projects will be built." Watch for the final order.
  • MIMitten Megastorage: The MPSC approved six BESS contracts totaling 1,332 MW — a rare clean-deck procurement. Meanwhile, ITC's two MISO-approved transmission projects (~$850M) are drawing eminent-domain pushback; a Republican-sponsored bill would expand landowner challenges.
  • MDPiedmont Pressure: The Maryland Piedmont Reliability Project files environmental and routing studies in March 2026 ahead of September public hearings; counties along the route are organizing against eminent-domain use.
  • GAPay to Plug In: The Georgia Public Service Commission's new rule requires data centers to fund their own upstream generation, transmission, and distribution costs — a model other PSCs are now studying.
  • NJDown the Shore Holds: A federal judge blocked a lawsuit seeking to halt a New Jersey offshore wind project — the rare opposition setback in a brutal year for Atlantic offshore.
